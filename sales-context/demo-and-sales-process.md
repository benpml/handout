# Demo and Sales Process

## Demo objective

Prove the full value loop in the fewest credible steps:

> Reusable master site → recipient personalization → Gmail sharing → buyer experience → engagement-informed follow-up.

Do not spend the majority of the demo touring settings or every content block. The most important product insight is that Handout changes the seller-to-buyer workflow, not merely how a page is styled.

## Pre-demo preparation

### Know the prospect’s motion

Choose one:

- Discovery follow-up.
- Demo recap.
- Pricing/packaging.
- Executive summary.
- Implementation plan.
- Agency proposal companion.
- Partner co-sell.

### Prepare a realistic master site

Use approved or clearly fictional demo content. Include:

- A concise title and subtitle.
- 2–4 logical pages/tabs.
- One account variable in a headline or body.
- One pain/use-case variable.
- One relevant proof element.
- One modeled CTA.
- Optional video/calendar only if it supports the story.

### Prepare recipient values

- Name.
- Company.
- Website/domain for logo.
- Relevant problem.
- Recommended next step.
- Booking/CTA URL.

### Prepare tracking proof

Use a safe demo recipient link and create:

- One site session.
- One tab switch.
- One button or supported link click.

Do not fabricate customer activity. Make clear that the events are demo actions.

### Confirm availability

Before a customer demo, verify:

- The app and public site are reachable.
- The site is published.
- The Gmail extension is installed if it will be shown.
- The correct workspace is active.
- Tracking is enabled.
- Internal-traffic suppression will not hide the demo, or use a known external test context.
- Recipient link and embed image resolve.
- No sensitive or real prospect data is exposed.

## Recommended 15-minute demo

### 1. Reframe the problem — 1 minute

> “After a good call, most sellers still make the buyer assemble the story from an email, a deck, and several links. Handout makes the follow-up one personalized destination and gives the seller explicit engagement signals.”

### 2. Show the buyer experience first — 2 minutes

Open the published recipient link.

Point out:

- Personalized recipient/company context.
- Clear narrative.
- Tabs/pages for different stakeholders.
- Supporting proof and media.
- One obvious next step.
- Responsive, branded presentation.

Why first: the buyer experience is the outcome. The editor only matters because it makes this outcome repeatable.

### 3. Show the reusable master — 3 minutes

Open the editor.

Demonstrate:

- Direct editing.
- Slash menu or one relevant block.
- Pages/tabs and sidebar.
- Variables in context.
- Preview/publish state.
- Saved/collaboration state where available.

Narrative:

> “This is one master site. The team updates the structure and approved content here; recipient-specific details stay in variables.”

Avoid building a full page live. Make one small change that illustrates speed and then undo or keep it within the demo plan.

### 4. Create the recipient — 3 minutes

Open Share.

Demonstrate:

- New recipient and Past recipients.
- Name, company, website/logo, and custom variables.
- Stable personalized link.
- Email embed preview.
- Editing values without creating a new page copy.

Narrative:

> “The recipient is not a cloned page. It is a stable link with recipient values layered onto the published master.”

### 5. Share from Gmail — 3 minutes

If the extension is available:

- Open a Gmail compose.
- Click Share a Handout.
- Select the site.
- Reuse/create the recipient.
- Preview.
- Insert email card or link.

State the privacy boundary:

> “The extension does not read the email body, subject, thread, or attachments. It uses the compose recipient only to help prepare the Handout when the seller chooses to act.”

If the extension is unavailable, use Copy embed or Copy link and say the compose extension availability will be confirmed separately.

### 6. Show engagement — 2 minutes

Open Tracking.

Demonstrate:

- Sessions versus Events.
- Recipient and site context.
- Duration/activity.
- Explicit tab/link/button event.
- Broad device/location if present.

Narrative:

> “This tells the rep that the recipient link generated a session and which supported actions occurred. It is not replay and it does not use a persistent device identity.”

### 7. Tie to their workflow — 1 minute

> “For your team, the pilot would use this same loop after [specific motion]: one approved [site], personalized by the rep, sent from [Gmail/copy link], with [specific CTA] and [specific success measure].”

## 30-minute discovery demo

### Suggested agenda

- 5 minutes: current workflow and pain confirmation.
- 15 minutes: end-to-end product flow.
- 5 minutes: trust, availability, and workflow fit.
- 5 minutes: pilot scope and decision process.

### Interactive prompts

- “Which section would your champion forward internally?”
- “What are the three values that change by account?”
- “Which action would tell the rep to follow up differently?”
- “Who should own the master site?”
- “What would make this too heavy for reps?”

## Demo anti-patterns

- Starting with the sites table and narrating every navigation item.
- Spending ten minutes on formatting.
- Calling a recipient a cloned page.
- Claiming exact visitor identity from a default link.
- Calling session detail a recording or replay.
- Interpreting a Slack preview as definitive human activity.
- Demonstrating unconfirmed roadmap items.
- Using real customer data without permission.
- Showing a non-published draft as if it were live.
- Skipping the Gmail or share step and leaving the workflow abstract.
- Ending without a specific pilot motion.

## Sales process stages

### Stage 1: Problem confirmation

Exit criteria:

- Named follow-up motion.
- Current process understood.
- Pain has measurable frequency or consequence.
- Basic public-link/Gmail fit.

### Stage 2: Workflow proof

Exit criteria:

- Prospect saw the end-to-end flow.
- Recipient variables identified.
- CTA and buyer experience agreed.
- User/champion interest confirmed.

### Stage 3: Pilot design

Exit criteria:

- One master site.
- Pilot cohort.
- Baseline and success metrics.
- Owner and decision date.
- Security/extension review path.

### Stage 4: Trust and commercial validation

Exit criteria:

- Product availability confirmed.
- Security/privacy questions answered with current evidence.
- Seat/pricing model accepted.
- Procurement and legal path known.

### Stage 5: Pilot execution

Exit criteria:

- Site approved and published.
- Sellers trained on recipient creation/share.
- Real target opportunities selected.
- Usage and outcomes reviewed weekly.

### Stage 6: Expansion decision

Exit criteria:

- Adoption and outcome evidence.
- Rollout owner.
- Additional sites/motions prioritized.
- Seat plan and enablement motion agreed.

## Pilot template

### Scope

- 1 sales motion.
- 1 reusable Handout.
- 3–10 users.
- 20–50 appropriate recipient sends, depending on deal volume.
- 30 days or enough time to observe a real next-step cycle.

### Inputs

- Approved narrative and proof.
- CTA/booking destination.
- Recipient-variable list.
- Current baseline.
- Security/brand approval.

### Success measures

- Median time from trigger to send.
- Percentage of eligible follow-ups sent through Handout.
- Seller ease-of-use feedback.
- Recipient sessions and modeled actions.
- Next-step timing and conversion versus a reasonable baseline.
- Buyer/champion feedback.
- Number of duplicated assets avoided.

### Review cadence

- Kickoff and content workshop.
- Midpoint seller feedback.
- Final evidence review and rollout decision.

## Business-case framing

### Efficiency

Estimate avoidable preparation time and support time. Use conservative assumptions.

### Conversion/velocity

Treat as a pilot hypothesis:

- Clearer, faster follow-up may improve the chance and speed of the next meeting.
- Recipient activity may improve follow-up timing.

Do not forecast guaranteed revenue from unvalidated conversion assumptions.

### Content governance

Value can also come from:

- Fewer stale decks.
- Faster playbook updates.
- Higher use of approved proof.
- Reduced enablement/design requests.

## Post-demo follow-up

The ideal post-demo follow-up is itself a Handout for the prospect, containing:

- What was heard.
- The use case demonstrated.
- Relevant capability summary.
- Trust/availability caveats.
- Proposed pilot.
- Clear next action.

This is high-leverage dogfooding because it proves the buyer experience using the prospect’s own decision.
