# Ideal Customer Profiles

## ICP philosophy

Handout is strongest where the cost of weak follow-up is high, the seller repeatedly assembles similar material, and engagement signals change the next action.

The ideal customer is not simply “anyone who sends a link.” The product becomes valuable when a team has all or most of these conditions:

- B2B selling with identifiable accounts and stakeholders.
- Consultative or considered purchases rather than instant self-serve transactions.
- Multiple pieces of collateral or context after a conversation.
- A recurring need to personalize the narrative.
- A meaningful call to action: book a meeting, review pricing, align on next steps, invite stakeholders, or evaluate implementation.
- A seller who benefits from knowing whether the buyer opened and interacted with the material.
- Enough sales volume that manually building bespoke follow-up is costly.
- Enough deal value that a better buyer experience can justify a per-seat tool.

## Primary ICP: document-heavy B2B sellers

### Definition

B2B sales teams selling a product or service that requires discovery, explanation, proof, and follow-up. They currently send decks, PDFs, links, recap emails, proposals, case studies, security material, implementation notes, and booking links across multiple touchpoints.

### Strong company characteristics

- Roughly 5–200 quota-carrying or customer-facing sellers, though a founder-led team can also fit.
- Sales motion includes discovery and demo or consultation.
- Typical deal has multiple stakeholders or an internal champion.
- Sales cycle is long enough for follow-up quality and timing to matter.
- Average contract value is large enough that a modest conversion or velocity gain is meaningful.
- Team sells a product with visual proof, differentiated workflow, business case, or implementation story.
- Email is a primary follow-up channel.
- Sellers work from a repeatable playbook but still need account-specific tailoring.

### Strong operational symptoms

- Reps copy old decks or Google Docs for each account.
- Follow-up quality varies widely by rep.
- Sales enablement cannot tell which version is being sent.
- Reps paste five or more links into emails.
- Prospects ask for “something I can share internally.”
- Champions struggle to retell the story to other stakeholders.
- Sellers do not know whether the buyer reviewed what was sent.
- Existing digital sales room software feels too heavy or expensive for the fast path.
- Generic landing pages are managed by marketing and too slow to personalize.

### Why Handout fits

- One reusable master site replaces account-specific copies.
- Variables preserve personalization without fragmenting content.
- Multi-page structure lets the seller organize a complex story compactly.
- Gmail insertion makes the behavior easy enough to become habitual.
- Recipient-level activity helps sellers time and tailor follow-up.

## ICP 1: B2B SaaS sales teams

### Best-fit subsegments

- Horizontal or vertical SaaS with demo-led sales.
- Mid-market and enterprise sales teams.
- Developer tools, workflow tools, data products, infrastructure, security, HR, finance, and operations software where buyers need education and proof.
- Startups building a repeatable outbound and follow-up motion.

### Common Handout use cases

- Post-discovery recap.
- Post-demo recap with relevant workflows and video.
- Personalized solution overview.
- Pricing and packaging explanation.
- Security/implementation hub.
- Mutual action plan or evaluation plan.
- Champion enablement for internal sharing.
- Executive summary before a decision meeting.

### Buying signals

- Team is formalizing sales enablement.
- New sales leader wants consistent follow-up.
- Reps are creating many one-off decks.
- Company is moving upmarket and facing more stakeholders.
- Deal reviews reveal unclear next steps or poor buyer engagement.
- Team is already using Gmail and lightweight productivity tools heavily.

### Likely buyer

- VP Sales or Head of Sales.
- Revenue Operations leader.
- Sales Enablement leader.
- Founder/CEO for an earlier-stage company.

## ICP 2: professional services, agencies, and consultancies

### Best-fit subsegments

- Design and development agencies.
- Strategy and operations consultancies.
- Recruiting and talent firms.
- Specialized marketing agencies.
- Fractional executive or advisory businesses.
- Implementation partners and systems integrators.

### Common Handout use cases

- Discovery recap and recommended approach.
- Personalized capabilities overview.
- Proposal companion site.
- Scope, timeline, team, and proof in one destination.
- Case study collection tailored to the buyer’s problem.
- Next-step checklist and booking action.
- Partner co-selling page with duo branding.

### Why this segment is attractive

- Sales is document-heavy and relationship-driven.
- The buyer often forwards material internally.
- The service is hard to reduce to a generic product page.
- Each opportunity needs personalization, but the underlying methodology and proof repeat.
- A polished experience affects perceived quality and trust.

### Qualification caution

Avoid highly bespoke operational businesses where every proposal requires complex pricing tables, legal redlines, e-signature, resource scheduling, or a full client portal. Handout can complement those systems but should not be positioned as replacing them.

## ICP 3: founder-led and early-stage sales teams

### Best-fit profile

- Founder or small sales team selling a high-value B2B product.
- Strong product knowledge but limited design or enablement resources.
- Repeatedly customizes follow-up for strategic prospects.
- Needs a polished output without waiting on marketing.

### Why Handout fits

- Fast creation and reusable structure.
- Free drafting before publishing.
- Personalization for named early customers.
- A credible, shareable artifact for champions and investors’ portfolio referrals.
- Lightweight workflow without a heavy enterprise implementation.

### Expansion path

Land with the founder or first sellers, then expand to the growing sales team through shared patterns, recipient workflows, and per-seat Core billing.

## ICP 4: revenue enablement and RevOps-led teams

### Best-fit profile

- Team already has content but lacks a consistent delivery experience.
- Enablement wants to standardize narratives and proof while preserving seller flexibility.
- RevOps wants observable handoffs and less manual process.
- The company has enough sellers that page duplication and content drift are becoming a governance problem.

### Use cases

- Approved post-demo template.
- Segment-specific buyer site.
- Standardized executive summary.
- Reusable implementation and security pages.
- New-hire follow-up playbook.
- AI-generated recipient experiences using controlled source content.

### Key value

Handout can become a governed last-mile delivery layer: centralized story, recipient-specific values, and observable buyer actions.

### Important caveat

Do not imply mature enterprise governance, audit logs, SSO, SCIM, or CRM-native workflow unless separately verified. Lead with content consistency and workflow, then scope requirements honestly.

## ICP 5: partnership, channel, and co-selling teams

### Best-fit profile

- Partnerships generate opportunities that need shared context.
- Two companies need a lightweight co-branded follow-up.
- Partner managers repeatedly assemble solution and next-step material.

### Use cases

- Co-branded account overview.
- Joint solution narrative.
- Referral follow-up.
- Partner enablement page.
- Event or campaign follow-up by account.

### Why Handout fits

- Page-title/logo treatments can represent partnership.
- Variables tailor the account, joint value, and CTA.
- Stable recipient links support forwarding.
- Engagement signals help the owner coordinate follow-up.

## Secondary ICPs

These can fit, but require a sharper use case:

### Customer success and expansion teams

Use for onboarding summaries, success plans, expansion proposals, QBR follow-up, and executive alignment. The product is sales-centered, so do not position it as a full customer-success platform.

### Recruiting and talent sales

Use for role/company pitches, candidate or client recaps, and engagement with a curated opportunity. Confirm privacy expectations and avoid putting sensitive candidate data into public pages.

### Events and field marketing follow-up

Use for named-account post-event follow-up with relevant sessions, proof, and booking links. Generic campaign landing pages remain a marketing-tool use case.

### Investor or fundraising outreach

The mechanics fit personalized narrative plus engagement, but the product language and roadmap are sales-oriented. Treat as adjacent, not primary.

## Account fit signals

### High-intent signals

- “We send a deck after every demo.”
- “Our reps build their own follow-up docs.”
- “Buyers keep asking for one place with all the material.”
- “We need something our champion can forward internally.”
- “We don’t know whether anyone looked at what we sent.”
- “Our current deal rooms are too much for most opportunities.”
- “Marketing cannot build a custom page for every account.”
- “We want AI to help create personalized follow-up, but we need control.”
- “Our sellers live in Gmail.”

### Technographic or workflow signals

- Gmail/Google Workspace is central to the sales workflow.
- Team uses Notion, Google Docs, Canva, presentation tools, or PDF proposals for follow-up.
- Team has a CRM but still assembles buyer-facing material manually.
- Company has a rich library of videos, case studies, product links, calendars, and proof points.
- Team is experimenting with AI sales agents or MCP-enabled workflows.

### Organizational signals

- New VP Sales, RevOps, or Enablement hire.
- Move from founder-led to repeatable sales.
- Move from SMB to mid-market/enterprise.
- New product launch or new segment playbook.
- Increased multi-threading and buying-committee complexity.
- Rebrand or content-governance initiative.

## Negative ICP and disqualification

### Low-value transactional sales

Poor fit when buyers purchase immediately from a standard product page and sellers do not conduct meaningful follow-up.

### Primarily inbound marketing use case

Poor fit when the need is SEO, high-volume anonymous traffic, forms, A/B testing, ad campaigns, or marketing automation. Handout is designed for seller-shared buyer experiences, not a broad marketing CMS.

### Requirement for legal proposal workflow

Poor fit when the primary need is quoting, CPQ, e-signature, contract redlining, or legally binding proposal acceptance.

### Requirement for heavy enterprise deal rooms

Poor fit when the organization requires complex file permissions, buyer authentication, procurement workflows, SSO/SCIM, advanced audit logs, and formal enterprise certifications from day one.

### Requirement for invasive identity or replay

Poor fit when the buyer insists on session replay, persistent visitor identity, anonymous deanonymization, message pixels, or arbitrary browser capture. These conflict with Handout’s current privacy design.

### Primarily Outlook/Teams workflow

Potential fit for copied links, but the implemented compose extension targets Gmail. Do not pitch a native Outlook workflow unless it exists separately.

### Highly regulated or sensitive content

Proceed cautiously where public recipient links would contain health data, financial account data, export-controlled data, credentials, or other regulated information. Handout is not established here as a compliance-heavy content repository.

## ICP prioritization score

Score each dimension from 0–2:

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Follow-up frequency | Occasional | Weekly | Daily/high volume |
| Deal complexity | Transactional | Some explanation | Multi-step/multi-stakeholder |
| Content fragmentation | One link | Several assets | Repeated link/attachment bundles |
| Personalization need | None | Name/company | Account problem, proof, CTA |
| Engagement value | Nice to know | Helpful | Changes timing/prioritization |
| Gmail fit | Not used | Mixed | Primary seller inbox |
| Standardization need | One seller | Small team | Enablement/governance problem |
| Buyer forwarding | Rare | Sometimes | Critical champion behavior |

Interpretation:

- **13–16:** Excellent fit. Prioritize a live workflow demo and pilot.
- **9–12:** Good fit. Anchor on the highest-friction use case.
- **5–8:** Situational. Prove a narrow workflow before broader positioning.
- **0–4:** Likely poor fit or premature.

## Recommended initial market focus

The most focused starting segment is:

> Founder-led, mid-market, and enablement-led B2B teams selling a document-heavy product or service through Gmail, where reps repeatedly send personalized post-call material and need a lightweight alternative to generic decks, link dumps, or heavy digital sales rooms.

This segment maps directly to the implemented strengths and avoids requiring unsupported enterprise features.
