# Discovery and Qualification

## Discovery objective

Determine whether Handout can improve a specific, recurring seller-to-buyer follow-up workflow. Do not run a generic “website builder” discovery. Anchor on the moment after a call, demo, proposal, event, or buyer request when the seller must assemble and send useful context.

## Opening frame

> I’d like to understand what happens between the moment a buyer asks for more information and your team’s next conversation—what reps assemble, how buyers consume it, and what you can see after it is sent.

## Core discovery areas

### 1. Trigger and frequency

- What customer interaction usually creates the need for follow-up?
- How often does a seller send this kind of material?
- Which stages or motions are the most important?
- Is this behavior consistent across reps or account-specific?
- How many opportunities per seller per month would use it?

Listen for:

- Daily or weekly repetition.
- High-value moments after discovery/demo.
- A clear motion that can become a pilot.

### 2. Current process

- Walk me through the last time a prospect said, “send me more information.”
- What did the rep send?
- Where did the content come from?
- How many tools or files were involved?
- How long did it take from call end to sending?
- Who had to help?
- Does the rep start from a template or copy an old asset?

Listen for:

- Deck copies.
- Long email assembly.
- Link hunting.
- Marketing bottlenecks.
- Content drift.

### 3. Personalization

- What actually changes by account or stakeholder?
- Is personalization mostly name/company, or does the narrative, proof, CTA, or recommendation change?
- How do you keep those changes consistent across a deck or page?
- How often do reps accidentally send stale or incorrect account information?
- Do different stakeholders at the same account need different framing?

Listen for variable candidates:

- Name/company.
- Pain point.
- Use case.
- Industry proof.
- Recommended plan.
- Business case.
- Booking or next-step link.

### 4. Buyer experience

- What does the buyer have to open or navigate?
- Can the champion easily forward the material internally?
- Which stakeholders need different levels of detail?
- What questions recur because the follow-up was unclear?
- Is there one obvious next action?
- How does the experience work on mobile?

Listen for:

- Attachment friction.
- Link overload.
- Champion retelling burden.
- Lack of narrative structure.

### 5. Engagement and follow-up

- What can the seller see after sending?
- Which signals would change the timing or content of follow-up?
- Do reps follow up on a schedule, intuition, or evidence?
- How do you distinguish buyer engagement from bot or internal activity today?
- Would explicit actions such as opening, tab changes, or CTA clicks be sufficient, or is replay/visitor deanonymization mandatory?

Listen for:

- Clear next actions tied to engagement.
- Pain around no visibility.
- Requirements incompatible with Handout’s privacy model.

### 6. Team consistency and governance

- Who creates the base material?
- How do you update it across the team?
- Can reps change anything, or should some content stay controlled?
- How do new hires learn what to send?
- How many duplicate versions exist?
- Who owns the buyer experience: sales, enablement, marketing, or RevOps?

### 7. Workflow and integration

- Which inbox do sellers use?
- How important is Gmail-native insertion?
- What CRM or sales tools surround the workflow?
- Would copy/paste links be acceptable for a first pilot?
- Is AI-assisted generation a requirement or an opportunity?
- Who would operate the MCP/agent workflow?

### 8. Security and compliance

- What kind of information would be placed on the site?
- Are public recipient links acceptable?
- Is buyer authentication required?
- Are there prohibited data types?
- What retention, DPA, residency, certification, SSO, or audit requirements exist?
- Does browser-extension installation require IT approval?

## Quantification questions

### Seller time

- Average minutes to assemble one follow-up.
- Follow-ups per seller per week.
- Number of sellers.
- Percentage that can use a reusable master site.
- Time spent by design, marketing, or solutions consultants.

Simple annual time estimate:

`minutes per follow-up × follow-ups per week × sellers × 48 working weeks ÷ 60`

Do not assume all time becomes saved capacity. Ask what portion is realistically avoidable.

### Content governance

- Number of duplicate decks/docs/pages.
- Frequency of messaging or pricing updates.
- Incidents caused by stale material.
- Time to launch a new sales play.

### Revenue impact

- Number of opportunities in the target motion.
- Current next-meeting conversion.
- Median days from call to next step.
- Percentage of deals where a champion forwards material.
- Cost or value of one additional qualified next meeting.

Use these as pilot measures, not guaranteed outcomes.

## Qualification framework: LIGHT

### L — Last-mile pain

Is there a recurring problem assembling and delivering post-call material?

Strong:

- Multiple assets, long emails, duplicate pages, or poor buyer clarity.

Weak:

- A single standard link already solves the need.

### I — Identifiable recipient motion

Are sites sent to named accounts or stakeholders where recipient-level links are useful?

Strong:

- Account-based or consultative selling.

Weak:

- Anonymous high-volume marketing traffic.

### G — Guided next step

Is there a meaningful action the site should drive?

Strong:

- Book review, align executive, assess implementation, choose package, complete evaluation step.

Weak:

- Passive brochure with no clear decision.

### H — Habit and scale

Will the workflow happen often enough to become a seller habit and justify per-seat value?

Strong:

- Weekly/daily use across a team.

Weak:

- One-off annual use.

### T — Trust and technical fit

Does the public-link, Gmail, and privacy-conscious event model satisfy requirements?

Strong:

- Public buyer content, Gmail, event-level insights accepted.

Weak:

- Mandatory buyer authentication, Outlook-only native workflow, session replay, or strict enterprise controls not currently available.

Score each 0–2. A 7–10 score is a strong pilot candidate; 4–6 needs a narrow proof; 0–3 is likely poor fit.

## Qualification by MEDDPICC-style concerns

### Metrics

- Time to create follow-up.
- Adoption of approved content.
- Next-meeting rate.
- Sales-cycle days.
- CTA actions.
- Duplicate asset reduction.

### Economic buyer

Typically VP Sales, CRO, founder, agency owner, or services leader.

### Decision criteria

- Seller speed.
- Buyer presentation quality.
- Personalization model.
- Gmail workflow.
- Engagement data and privacy.
- Ease of rollout.
- Pricing.
- Security and compliance.

### Decision process

- User validation with sellers.
- Manager/enablement sign-off.
- Extension/security review.
- Pilot.
- Commercial approval and rollout.

### Paper process

Confirm:

- Procurement threshold.
- Security questionnaire.
- DPA/legal terms.
- Browser-extension policy.
- Payment and seat management.

### Identify pain

Use the actual last follow-up as evidence. Avoid abstract “content chaos” when the team cannot produce a concrete example.

### Champion

Needs workflow ownership, credibility with sellers, and access to a pilot cohort.

### Competition

Usually the status quo: deck + email + links, generic landing page, document hub, or an existing digital sales room.

## High-value follow-up questions

- If the workflow were perfect, what would the seller do in the first five minutes after a call?
- What would the buyer see when they open the email?
- Which recipient-specific details should never require a new page copy?
- What action would tell the seller the deal is moving?
- Which current tool would remain the source of truth?
- What would prevent sellers from adopting this?
- What has to be true for security to approve it?
- Which single sales motion would make the best 30-day pilot?

## Discovery red flags

- Prospect cannot name a specific follow-up workflow.
- Need is primarily marketing traffic acquisition.
- Buyer insists on anonymous visitor deanonymization.
- Buyer requires session replay.
- Buyer needs contract/CPQ/e-signature rather than presentation.
- Buyer expects CRM sync or enrichment in the initial evaluation.
- Buyer requires an Outlook extension immediately.
- Public links are categorically prohibited.
- Required certifications or SSO are non-negotiable and unavailable.
- Deal value/frequency cannot support even a lightweight per-seat tool.

## Mutual pilot design

### Select one motion

Examples:

- Post-demo recap.
- Pricing follow-up.
- Agency proposal companion.
- Executive summary.

### Select one reusable site

Start with existing approved content and a clear CTA.

### Select a small cohort

3–10 sellers is usually enough to expose usability and adoption issues without requiring a broad rollout.

### Define baseline

- Current creation time.
- Current follow-up format.
- Current next-step conversion/time.
- Current seller and buyer feedback.

### Define success

- Site created and approved.
- Sellers can personalize and send without support.
- Target percentage of applicable opportunities use Handout.
- Measured change in time-to-send.
- Sessions/actions captured where tracking is enabled.
- Qualitative buyer response.
- Agreed commercial/rollout decision date.

### Avoid bad pilot metrics

- Raw page views alone.
- Unique visitors, which the product does not claim.
- Uncontrolled comparison across unrelated deal types.
- Guaranteed revenue outcome in a small sample.

## Next-step discipline

Every discovery should end with one of these:

- Live workflow demo using the prospect’s use case.
- Content/workflow workshop for the pilot site.
- Security and extension review.
- Commercial review.
- Explicit disqualification.

Avoid “send more information” without first identifying which Handout use case the information should support.
