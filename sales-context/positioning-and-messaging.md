# Positioning and Messaging

## Recommended positioning statement

For B2B sales teams that repeatedly assemble personalized follow-up from decks, docs, links, and emails, Handout is a personalized sales-site platform that turns one reusable buyer experience into recipient-specific links with actionable engagement signals. Unlike generic page builders, static collateral, or heavy digital sales rooms, Handout is designed around the seller’s fast path: build once, personalize, share from Gmail, and follow up based on explicit buyer activity.

## Category

Primary category:

- Personalized sales-site platform.

Supporting category language:

- Buyer-site builder for B2B sales.
- Personalized sales one-pager.
- Lightweight digital sales room.
- Sales follow-up experience platform.

Avoid leading with:

- “Landing-page builder” — sounds marketing-led and generic.
- “Microsite CMS” — emphasizes publishing infrastructure rather than sales outcomes.
- “Email tracking” — narrows the product and suggests pixels/surveillance.
- “Session replay” — false for the current event-only tracking model.
- “AI website generator” — AI is an enabling workflow, not the whole product.

## Core narrative

### The old way

After a good call, sellers send a long email, a deck, and a collection of links. The buyer has to assemble the story. The seller has to rebuild the package for every account and then guess whether anyone engaged.

### The new way

The team maintains one polished buyer site for the motion. The seller personalizes the relevant values, inserts a recipient-specific card from Gmail, and gives the buyer one clear place to understand the story and take the next step. Handout shows the seller the sessions and modeled actions that follow.

### The value

- The buyer gets clarity.
- The seller moves faster.
- The team stays consistent.
- Follow-up becomes evidence-driven.

## Message hierarchy

### Message 1: One clear buyer destination

Turn scattered follow-up into one responsive, forwardable place for the account’s story, proof, resources, and next step.

Proof points:

- Multi-page/tab structure.
- Sidebar links and next-step buttons.
- Rich blocks: text, lists, tables, proof, images, video, calendar, testimonials, logos, and CTAs.
- Shared Preview and published rendering.

### Message 2: Personalize without duplicating

Create one master site and personalize the recipient name, company, problem, proof, CTA, logo, booking URL, or other variables without creating a detached page copy.

Proof points:

- Variables with defaults.
- Recipient-specific overrides.
- Stable recipient links.
- Past-recipient reuse.
- Master content remains authoritative.

### Message 3: Send where sellers work

Create or reuse a recipient and insert a linked card or descriptive link directly from Gmail.

Proof points:

- Gmail compose toolbar action.
- Published-site search.
- New/past recipient flow.
- Preview, insert email card, insert link, and copy-link actions.
- No email body, thread, or attachment reading.

### Message 4: Follow up on explicit engagement

See sessions and modeled actions such as site opens, supported link/button clicks, and tab changes, attributed to a recipient when a recipient link is used.

Proof points:

- Sessions and Events views.
- Recipient, site, duration, activity count, time, broad device, and approximate location.
- Modeled element labels owned by the server.
- Retention and internal-traffic controls.

### Message 5: Stay useful without becoming invasive

Handout collects first-party activity needed for sales follow-up while excluding replay, raw IP storage, persistent device IDs, typed values, arbitrary DOM capture, and cross-session visitor tracking.

## Elevator pitches

### 10-second pitch

Handout turns sales follow-up into a personalized buyer site you can send from Gmail and track by recipient.

### 30-second pitch

Handout helps B2B sellers replace decks, attachments, and link-heavy follow-up with one polished buyer site. The team builds the master experience once, the rep personalizes it for each recipient, inserts it from Gmail, and sees privacy-safe sessions and explicit actions after it is opened.

### 60-second pitch

Most sales teams still answer “send me more information” with a long email, a deck, and five links. That creates work for the seller and forces the buyer to assemble the story. Handout gives the team one reusable buyer site for a motion—discovery follow-up, demo recap, pricing, implementation, or executive alignment. Reps personalize variables such as the recipient, company, pain point, proof, and CTA, then create a stable recipient link and insert a visual card directly from Gmail. When the buyer engages, the rep sees sessions and supported actions without session replay or persistent visitor tracking. The result is faster follow-up, a clearer buyer experience, and better timing for the next conversation.

### Sales leader

### Enablement

> Put approved narrative, proof, and next steps into a reusable site reps will actually send. Keep the master current while letting sellers tailor the account-specific layer.

### RevOps

> Replace page copies and opaque follow-up with a structured site, recipient, and event model that can be operated through the product and agent/MCP workflows.

### Founder

> Package the way you sell into a polished, reusable buyer site—then tailor it for every important prospect in minutes.

### Agency/consultant

> Turn discovery into a clear, personalized client experience with your approach, proof, timeline, and next step in one forwardable link.

## Outcome language

Use these phrases when supported by the prospect’s problem:

- Reduce time from call to polished follow-up.
- Make follow-up easier for the buyer to understand and forward.
- Reuse approved content without looking generic.
- Reduce duplicated pages, decks, and version drift.
- Give champions a clearer internal story.
- Put the right proof and next step in context.
- Prioritize follow-up based on recipient-site activity.
- Improve seller consistency without removing personalization.
- Create a privacy-conscious alternative to replay-heavy engagement tools.

Avoid unproven outcome claims such as:

- “Increase close rates by X%.”
- “Cut sales cycles in half.”
- “Guaranteed buyer engagement.”
- “Know exactly who viewed every page.”
- “Replace your CRM.”

## Message by trigger

### “We send decks after demos”

> Keep the core story reusable, but give each buyer one personalized, responsive destination with the relevant proof and next action. You can update the master instead of creating another deck copy.

### “Our buyers need something to forward”

> A recipient-specific Handout gives your champion one stable link that preserves the narrative and supporting detail when it moves to another stakeholder.

### “We do not know what happens after we send”

> Handout shows sessions and explicit actions on the buyer site. It does not claim persistent person identity or record the screen; it gives you enough evidence to time and tailor the next step.

### “Our digital sales room is overkill”

> Handout is intentionally lightweight. It focuses on the fast follow-up path rather than a large authenticated deal portal.

### “Our reps will not use another tool”

> The goal is to reuse a ready site and create a recipient from Gmail, not ask every rep to design a page from scratch.

### “We want AI personalization”

> Handout exposes structured MCP tools for site creation, validation, recipient personalization, publishing, URLs, and tracking reads. The agent can accelerate production while the site model, permissions, and publish step remain controlled.

## Messaging by funnel stage

### Top of funnel

Lead with the recognizable pain:

- Link-heavy follow-up.
- Deck copies.
- Buyer confusion.
- No engagement visibility.

### Discovery

Quantify:

- Frequency of follow-up.
- Time spent assembling it.
- Number of assets.
- Importance of buyer forwarding.

### Demo

Show:

- Reusable site.
- Recipient variables.
- Gmail insertion.
- Buyer experience.
- Resulting events.

### Business case

Tie value to:

- Seller time.
- Content governance.
- Adoption of approved proof.
- Next-meeting velocity.
- Conversion or cycle-time pilot metrics.

### Security/procurement

Lead with precise boundaries, not vague “privacy-first” language. Explain what is collected, what is not, extension permissions, retention choices, and public-link implications.

## Language guide

### Preferred terms

- Site or buyer site.
- Recipient.
- Recipient link.
- Personalize.
- Variables.
- Sessions.
- Activity or events.
- Explicit/modelled actions.
- Slack preview signal.
- Public link.
- Master site.

### Terms to explain or avoid

- Variant: acceptable for API/internal discussion; prefer recipient in buyer-facing conversation.
- One-pager: useful shorthand, but Handout can contain multiple pages/tabs.
- Tracking: explain the privacy boundary immediately.
- Watch: a current UI label may appear for session detail, but do not describe it as replay.
- Unique visitor: do not use.
- Identified visitor: use only when attribution comes from a recipient-specific link and state that explicitly.
- Shared on Slack: qualify as a preview/unfurl signal.

## Proof hierarchy

Use proof in this order:

1. Live product workflow.
2. Current repository-defined behavior.
3. Prospect-provided baseline and pilot outcome.
4. Approved customer evidence, when available.
5. Product direction clearly labeled as direction.

Never substitute fictional testimonials, implied customers, or roadmap items for proof.

## Positioning guardrails

- Handout complements the CRM; it does not replace it.
- Handout complements CPQ/contracts; it does not replace them.
- Handout can be lighter than a digital sales room; do not claim every DSR capability.
- Handout is more sales-specific than a generic page builder; do not claim unrestricted web design.
- Handout adds engagement context to a public buyer site; it does not deanonymize the web.
- Handout’s AI story is structured and controlled; it is not autonomous publishing without permission.
